Well would you look at that. Blog post number 100. 10 years writing on this small corner of the internet. Writing this week's Retrospectiva from my favourite place: the airplane. 

Happy Easter if you celebrate. With clients spread across time zones, I didn't really have the time to. Next week I'm going to be at AI Engineer London. Email me if you're coming too and let's nerd out. 

Let's get to it. 

*Experimenting with a slightly different format this week* 

## Using

**Pi.dev**: After watching this talk on how to use Pi, I was pretty much (re-) sold on the harness. It's become my main driver for the past month. I've created a nice collection of skills (some private), extensions, and even adapted my favourite color scheme - modus - to it. An agent harness is a bit like an editor. I want it mine. I want to `/preview` a longer agent plan in a nice web page. I want to answer questions in a nice interface with `/answer`. I want the theme to adapt to my MacOs system theme. I want to use it with ANY model under the sun. 

**Codex**: Some things need my full attention. Others don't. When its a quick fix, a breaking test, a simpler analysis, or something I can run in parallel, the Codex app from OpenAI has been my go to. The interface is simple and straightforward, you can preview diffs, open in a terminal, create PRs, all from the same place. I tend to throw at it the smaller parallel things I don't want to think too much about. 

**Hevy**: I [continue to run](/blog/an-opinionated-running-dashboard.html). Not as much as I would like to. But a healthy amount still. But as you get older, weight lifting and strength becomes more and more important. I tend to go a couple of times a week to the gym. After freestyling my gym routine for a couple of years, I wanted something a bit more structured and personalized. After testing 3-4 apps, I landed on Hevy. It's paid, but not expensive. Well built, and is a great companion for the days at the gym. I have a couple of annoyances with it, but for the majority of my needs, it works. 

**Snapseed**: My inspiration to take [photos](/photos) comes and goes. More than a camera, I need a nice editing app to make that ugly Danish sky jut _a bit_ prettier. After everything became a subscription, I've resorted to DarkRoom for the majority of my editing needs. It works, but the loading times of my edited photos are just unbearable. I remember Snapseed as this small app I used for basic healing/editing. Well not anymore, Snapseed is great now! It's fully featured. Filters are nice but not over the top. Healing is there. All the controls I might need for mobile photo. Without costing a single cent. 


## Reading 

**Empire of AI - Karen Hao**: Allegra is going through a small sleep regression, so I tested a new reading routine this month. One chapter per day, usually in the mornings. Ended up finishing this one. The book has some interesting tidbits about the history of OpenAI and some backstories on its key people. Some slightly deeper commentary on the ethics of AI and the infrastructure around. But a lot of TMZ style tech drama that I don't find either interesting or entertaining. 

**Inference Engineering - Philip Kiely**: Since I left this physical stack at home, I picked this one up on my [Palma](include link to palma blog post). Even though I'm early on it, I can definetly recommend it already. As work with larger and larger models, datasets, and infrastructure - it's a great field guide. 

**An unsolicited guide to good reasearch - Gaurig Gupta**: A great deck on how to do great research, and how to find interesting problems. I liked the framing, pragmatic and to the point.

**How Kimi, Cursor, and Chrome Train Agentic Models with RL - Phil Schmid**: An incredible round up of the Reinforcement Learning techniques some of the larger players are using to train models.

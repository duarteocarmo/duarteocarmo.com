Happy February! Just like that, we are close to wrapping up the first quarter of 2026. After quite some time roaming around, we finally flew back home to Copenhagen. It's cold, windy, and grey, but it's also calm, organized, and cozy. And most of all—it's home.

Another great thing about Denmark is the fact that it's one of the [flattest](https://www.instagram.com/p/C6oRHlVs4lK/) countries in Europe, which is perfect for increasing weekly running volume. And it's almost, almost time to ditch the running tights as well. Just a couple of months left.
## Using
Two interesting things have been changing in my workflow. The first my increasing usage of coding agents for everyday tasks. For repeatable things I've been using [agent skills](https://agentskills.io/home) more and more. Since I bounce around different agent CLIs quite a bit, I've been dropping all of them in `~./agents/skills` and reusing them across tools. For the past month, I've been using a mix of the [Codex app](https://developers.openai.com/codex/app/), and the shitty-but-not-so-shitty coding agent [Pi](https://shittycodingagent.ai/). The second interesting thing is the increasing capability of local coding agents. I completely replaced my GitHub Copilot subscription as well, with my own fork of [llama.vim](https://github.com/ggml-org/llama.vim), called [llama.lua](https://github.com/duarteocarmo/llama.lua).

## Reading

A couple of interesting long read recommendations for this month. The first is *[The Illustrated AlphaFold](https://elanapearl.github.io/blog/2024/the-illustrated-alphafold/)* by Elana P.Simon. She does a great illustrated rundown of the key inner workings of the famous algorithm from Google. Another recommendation is the latest article from Raschka: *[A Dream of Spring for Open-Weight LLMs: 10 Architectures from Jan-Feb 2026](https://sebastianraschka.com/blog/2026/a-dream-of-spring-for-open-weight.html)*. In face of the recent (alleged) news about Chinese labs [distilling](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.02531) models using the Anthropic API, it's interesting to note that in fact, architectures of large LLMs haven't really changed *that* much over time.

Pretty much finished with [LLMOps from Abi Aryan](https://www.amazon.com/LLMOps-Managing-Language-Models-Production/dp/1098154207). Interesting read to consolidate some of the ideas from everything I've been seeing in the field. This month I've picked up [Essentialism](https://gregmckeown.com/books/essentialism/) from Greg McKeown, and the [Art of Doing Science and Engineering](https://www.amazon.com/Art-Doing-Science-Engineering-Learning/dp/1732265178) from Richard Hamming. I'm not sure which one I'll continue with - certainly not both. We'll see which one draws me more, and I'll probably ditch the other. 

## Listening
I have three podcast recommendations for this month. Unsurprisingly - they are all very much about LLMs, AI, and the future of technology. But I'm sure you were already expecting that.

- [Jared Sleeper on Which Software Companies Will Survive the “SaaSpocalypse”](https://overcast.fm/+AA5AWNElGto) (Odd Lots): Software stocks have been dipping, why?
